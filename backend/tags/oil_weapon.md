# Oil Leverage

The Strait of Hormuz is blocked, but Saudi Arabia has a backdoor: **~3M bbl/day of spare production capacity** that can be exported through Yanbu port on the Red Sea — bypassing Hormuz entirely. This is the world's only meaningful oil buffer right now.

The question is not whether Saudi CAN pump more. It's whether they CHOOSE to. This makes it a leverage tool, not just a supply question.

## Injection

- **Gulf States** face a strategic choice: (1) Activate spare capacity through Yanbu to stabilize global oil prices, earning diplomatic goodwill and US gratitude, OR (2) Keep it offline and demand security guarantees first — "no safe passage for tankers, no extra barrels." Option 2 gives them maximum leverage but risks angering Washington.
- **US** desperately needs those barrels online to bring oil below $100 and relieve domestic political pressure. This gives Saudi Arabia enormous leverage over Washington — potentially extracting defense commitments, weapons deals, or post-war guarantees in exchange for turning on the taps.
- **Iran** understands that if Saudi spare capacity comes online via the Red Sea, the Hormuz blockade loses its economic bite — the pain shifts from global consumers to Iran alone. Preventing this (by threatening Yanbu via Houthi proxies) is therefore a top strategic priority for Tehran.
- **Market** reacts dramatically to any signal about Saudi spare capacity — a credible commitment to pump could drop oil $15-20/bbl overnight. Conversely, Saudi announcing "conditions not met, capacity stays offline" would send prices surging.
