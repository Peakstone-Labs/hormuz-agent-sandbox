# Market Patience Clock

**"No major crisis ever looks like a major crisis on its first day."** Markets initially give crises about 3 weeks of patience — one month of inventory, most can tolerate. **We are now in week 8.**

The Apr 7 ceasefire was the resolution attempt the market had been waiting for: Brent dropped from the $126 wartime peak to ~$87 on the announcement. But the deal has functionally collapsed — Islamabad Talks failed Apr 11-12, US imposed naval blockade Apr 13, Trump canceled the Witkoff/Kushner second round Apr 25. **Brent is back at $105+, up 16-18% on the week.** The market has now priced out the ceasefire optimism entirely; what remains is a binary distribution between "credible restart of talks" (Brent → $90) and "US strikes resume" (Brent → $120+).

Historical pattern: Wars rarely announce ceasefire dates at the start. People thought Russia-Ukraine would end in hours, then days, then weeks — it lasted years. The Vietnam War followed the same arc. **The "Apr 7 will fix it" hope has evaporated; the new dangerous consensus is "the May ceasefire window will fix it."**

## Injection

- **US** faces compounding pressure. Brent at $105+ creates stagflation risk; gas prices above $5/gallon are politically toxic with midterms approaching. The naval blockade is operationally expensive (3 CSGs, ~10 destroyers, Marines). Each day without a unified Iranian proposal is a day Trump's "wide-scale destruction" threat looks more like a bluff — but executing it would push Brent to $120+ and crystallize the political damage.
- **Gulf States** watch their GDP growth collapse from expected 3-4% to potentially negative. Capital flight accelerates. Iraq remains 95% Hormuz-dependent (~4M bpd) and effectively non-functional as an exporter. Saudi Aramco is running East-West Pipeline at full 7M bpd but Yanbu terminals are the new bottleneck. The wider regional summit framework they are pushing for is the only path to durable resolution.
- **Market** should model patience as a depleting resource that has been depleted. After week 3, each additional week without resolution causes disproportionate moves; we are now in week 8 with the deal officially failed twice (Apr 11-12 talks, Apr 25 cancellation). Institutional models have broken. Tanker insurance is at 10x+ pre-crisis levels; ~20,000 mariners and 2,000 ships are stranded per IMO. The Brent curve is in deep backwardation. Even if a deal is struck tomorrow, the risk premium will not normalize for quarters — inventory rebuilding plus structurally higher freight/insurance set a permanent new floor.
