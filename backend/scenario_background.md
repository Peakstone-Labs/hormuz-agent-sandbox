# Scenario Background: Strait of Hormuz Crisis
## As of April 10, 2026 (Simulation Start Date)

### Timeline of Key Events

**Feb 15-20:** Iran tripled oil exports and drew down storage, pre-positioning for disruption.

**Feb 28:** The United States and Israel launched surprise joint airstrikes across Iran, killing Supreme Leader Ali Khamenei, Defense Minister Aziz Nasirzadeh, IRGC commander Mohammad Pakpour, and Defence Council secretary Ali Shamkhani, along with four top intelligence officials. Dozens of civilian casualties reported.

**Mar 1:** Iran launched retaliatory missile and drone strikes against Israel, US military bases in the region, and Gulf states (Bahrain, Kuwait, Qatar, Saudi Arabia, UAE). The IRGC declared the Strait of Hormuz closed, threatening to set fire to any ship attempting transit. The oil tanker *Skylight* was struck north of Khasab, Oman, killing two Indian crew members.

**Mar 4:** IRGC formally enforced the Strait closure. Shipping traffic collapsed from 100+ vessels/day to near zero.

**Mar 8:** Brent crude surpassed $100/barrel for the first time in four years. Mojtaba Khamenei (son of the killed Supreme Leader) was elected as new Supreme Leader; IRGC and senior officials pledged allegiance.

**Mar 9:** President Trump announced intent to seize control of the Strait of Hormuz and warned Iran against laying mines.

**Mar 12:** Iran had carried out 21 confirmed attacks on merchant ships.

**Mar 19:** US Armed Forces began a military campaign to forcibly open the Strait.

**Mar 25:** Iran rejected the US 15-point peace proposal. Iran presented 5 conditions: (1) end of US-Israeli attacks on Iran and pro-Iranian forces, (2) mechanisms to prevent war resumption, (3) compensation for damages, (4) international recognition of Iranian sovereignty over the Strait, (5) [undisclosed].

**Mar 26:** Israel killed Iran's top naval commander, Alireza Tangsiri.

**Mar 27:** Brent crude hit $114/barrel after ceasefire negotiations failed and IRGC re-declared the Strait closed. Peak price during crisis: $126/barrel.

**Apr 1:** Kuwait International Airport struck by Iranian drone fire, causing large fire at fuel storage tanks. Trump claimed Iran's president requested a ceasefire; Iran denied this. Iran stated: "We are not seeking a ceasefire, we are seeking to end the war."

**Apr 3:** An F-15E was shot down over Iran (pilot rescued, second crew member missing). A second US plane (A-10) was struck near the Strait (pilot ejected and rescued). Iran struck Gulf refineries. Trump threatened to destroy Iranian bridges, power plants, and desalination facilities. 40 nations convened (without US/Israel) to discuss diplomatic reopening of the Strait. France declared forcible reopening "unrealistic."

**Apr 7:** Less than two hours before Trump's deadline for "wide-scale destruction" of Iranian infrastructure, the US and Iran agreed to a **two-week ceasefire**. Trump suspended bombing on the condition that Iran reopens the Strait of Hormuz. Iran put forward a **10-point proposal**, which Trump called "a workable basis on which to negotiate": (1) withdrawal of US combat forces from regional bases, (2) lifting of all sanctions, (3) release of frozen Iranian assets abroad, (4) full payment of Iran's war-related damages, (5) protocol for "controlled passage" through the Strait of Hormuz, plus additional points on non-aggression and recognition. Pakistan PM Shehbaz Sharif invited US and Iran to Islamabad for talks.

**Apr 8:** Ceasefire formally took effect, but Iran and Netanyahu confirmed **Lebanon is NOT covered**. Israel immediately escalated strikes on Hezbollah; Lebanon's health ministry reported hundreds killed or wounded. Brent spot prices fell ~$19.75 on the announcement, but the strait remained effectively closed: only a handful of vessels transited (vs. ~135/day normally), with Iran limiting ship counts and **charging tolls of over $1 million per vessel**. Some new attacks still hit Gulf targets.

**Apr 9:** Iran accused the US of violating the ceasefire and Iranian media reported Tehran was **considering pulling out of the deal** over Israel's Lebanon campaign. Iranian outlets said tanker traffic through the Strait was being suspended again. Despite the headlines, oil markets actually **sold off sharply** as the ceasefire announcement (and Israel's agreement to talks with Lebanon) priced in — Brent fell back from the >$120 spot peak. Saudi Arabia disclosed that attacks on its oil facilities have reduced production capacity by ~600,000 bpd and cut **East-West Pipeline throughput by ~700,000 bpd**, partially blunting the Yanbu workaround. Goldman Sachs warned that another month of Hormuz closure still means Brent averages above $100 throughout 2026. More than 600 vessels reported stuck in the Persian Gulf region.

**Apr 10 (Today):** VP JD Vance leading US negotiating team to Islamabad for talks with Iran, brokered by Pakistan. Ceasefire technically in effect but visibly fraying — Lebanon front active, Hormuz still effectively closed, both sides accusing the other of violations. The two-week clock (expires ~Apr 21) is the operative deadline.

### Current Market Conditions (April 10, 2026)
- **Brent Crude:** ~$95-97/barrel — sharply down from the $126 wartime peak and the $124 spot of Apr 8, as the ceasefire and Israel-Lebanon talks priced in. Still ~48% above year-ago levels and up ~4% over the past month.
- **WTI Crude:** trading alongside Brent in the mid-$90s; the usual Brent premium remains inverted
- **Gold:** ~$4,677/oz (up ~$1,640 YoY, approaching $5,000) — has not given back its safe-haven bid despite the oil pullback
- **Strait traffic:** Nominally "open" under ceasefire but functionally near-zero — only a handful of vessels transiting under Iranian-controlled passage with $1M+ tolls; 600+ vessels stranded in Gulf
- **Production shut in:** ~13 million bpd of Middle East output offline due to inability to export through Hormuz; Saudi East-West Pipeline throughput down ~700k bpd from facility attacks
- **Market read:** Prices are pricing in a *holding* ceasefire, not a *resolved* crisis — any collapse of the Apr 7 deal (especially via the Lebanon front) would snap Brent back toward $120+ within days

### Current Military Posture
- **Iran:** Two-week ceasefire with US in effect but threatened by Lebanon situation; Strait nominally reopened under Iranian "controlled passage" protocol with $1M+ tolls; new Supreme Leader Mojtaba Khamenei consolidating power; IRGC remains fully mobilized
- **US:** Strikes paused for two weeks; VP Vance leading negotiating team to Islamabad (Apr 10); Fifth Fleet still positioned in theater; Trump's threat to destroy Iranian bridges/power/desalination held in reserve as enforcement leverage
- **Israel:** NOT party to the ceasefire on the Lebanon front — actively escalating strikes against Hezbollah, killing hundreds; agreed to talks with Lebanon but vowed to continue fighting; restraint on Iran proper holding for now
- **Lebanon/Hezbollah:** Under intense Israeli bombardment since Apr 8; the carve-out is the single biggest threat to the wider US-Iran ceasefire holding
- **Gulf States:** Tentatively relieved by ceasefire but skeptical; Saudi Arabia continues rerouting exports through Red Sea port Yanbu (~4M bbl/day); UAE, Kuwait, Bahrain still recovering from missile/drone damage
- **Houthis:** Coordinating with Iran but maintaining independence; capable of disrupting Saudi Red Sea exports through Yanbu

### OPEC+ Status
- Production increases paused through year-end
- Iraq and Kuwait have involuntarily shut in production (cannot export through Hormuz)
- Saudi Arabia holds ~3 mb/d spare capacity — the world's only meaningful buffer
- Saudi increasing Red Sea exports through Yanbu to compensate
- Goldman Sachs: Brent stays above $100 throughout 2026 if Hormuz closure extends another month

### Diplomatic Status
- **Two-week US-Iran ceasefire in effect** (signed Apr 7, expires ~Apr 21)
- Islamabad talks beginning Apr 10, brokered by Pakistan PM Shehbaz Sharif; VP JD Vance leading US delegation
- Iran's 10-point proposal on the table; Trump called it "a workable basis"
- **Lebanon explicitly excluded** from ceasefire — Israel continues Hezbollah campaign, threatening collapse of the wider deal
- Iran accusing US of ceasefire violations; openly considering withdrawal
- 40-nation coalition (excluding US/Israel) had pushed for this diplomatic outcome
- France publicly opposed forcible reopening; the diplomatic track has narrowly prevailed — for now
